What is a Differential Equation?

An equation expressing a relation between functions, their derivatives, and the variables is called a differential equation.

Classification of Differential Equations

Ordinary Differential Equations

An ordinary differential equation is a differential equation in which the dependent variable (say \(y\)) depends only on one independent variable (say \(x\)).

Key Formula
\[\ F(x, y, y', y'', y'''.............) = 0\ \]
Example
\[\ \frac{dy}{dx} = \sin x + \cos x\ \]

Partial Differential Equations

A partial differential equation is a differential equation in which \(y\) depends on two or more independent variables \(x, t...\)

Key Formula
\[\ F\left(x, t, y, \frac{\partial y}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial y}{\partial t}, \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2}, \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2}, \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x \partial t}, ...\right) = 0\ \]
Example
\[\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial x} + t \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} = 2y\ \]

\(y = xt^2\)

Order and Degree of a Differential Equation

Order of the Differential Equation

The order of the highest derivative involved in a differential equation is called the order of the differential equation.

Degree of the Differential Equation

The degree of a differential equation is the degree or power of the highest ordered derivative present in the equation, after the equation is made free from radicals and fractions in respect of derivatives.

Example

Find order and degree of the following differential equations:

  1. \[\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0\]

    Order = 2, Degree = 1

  2. \[y = x \frac{dy}{dx} + a\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2\]

    Order = 1, Degree = 2

  3. \[\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + m^2y = 0\]

    Order = 2, Degree = 1

  4. \[y \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} - \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2 = x y^2 \log y\]

    Order = 2, Degree = 1

  5. \[1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2 = \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\]

    Order = 2, Degree = 1

  6. \[y = x \frac{dy}{dx} + a\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2}\]

    Order = 1, Degree = 2

Practical Approach to Differential Equations

Differential equations arise in many engineering and physical problems. The approach of an engineer to the study of differential equations has to be practical, and so, it consists of:

  1. Formation of a differential equation from the physical conditions, called modeling;
  2. Solution of a differential equation under the initial/boundary conditions; and
  3. The physical interpretation of the results.

Formation of a Differential Equation

We form a DE in order to eliminate the constants.

Order of the equation formed = number of constants to be eliminated.

Example

\(y = ax^2 + bx\)

\(\frac{dy}{dx} = 2ax + b\)

\(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 2a\)

\(a = \frac{1}{2} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\)

\(b = \frac{dy}{dx} - 2ax = \frac{dy}{dx} - x \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\)

Substituting values of \(a\) and \(b\) in \(y = ax^2 + bx\), we get

\(y = \frac{x^2}{2} \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + x\left(\frac{dy}{dx} - x \frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\right)\)

Order = 2, Degree = 1

Example

\(2y = Ae^{2x} + Be^{-2x}\)

\(y' = 2Ae^{2x} - 2Be^{-2x}\)

\(y'' = 4Ae^{2x} + 4Be^{-2x} = 4(Ae^{2x} + Be^{-2x}) = 4y\)

\(y'' - 4y = 0\)

Solution of a Differential Equation

A solution of a differential equation is the relation between the variables which satisfies the given differential equation.

Example

The solution of the differential equation \(\frac{dy}{dx} - 2y = 0\) is given by \(y = ce^{2x}\).

General Solution

The general solution of the differential equation is that in which the number of arbitrary constants is equal to the order of the given differential equation. \(y = ce^{2x}\)

Particular Solution

A particular solution is that solution which can be obtained from the general solution by giving particular values to the arbitrary constants. Ex. \(y = 2e^{2x}\)

Singular Solution

A singular solution is the solution of the differential equation which is neither a general solution nor a particular solution. Only some equations have a singular solution.

Example

Example: The solution of the DE \(\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2 - 4y = 0\) is

\(y = (x + a)^2\) is the general solution

\(y = x^2\) is the particular solution

The first order first degree differential equations are in the form of

Key Formula
\[F\left(x, y(x), \frac{dy}{dx}\right) = 0\]

Classification of First Order First Degree Differential Equations

  1. Variable separable equations
  2. Reducible to variable separable
  3. Homogeneous equations
  4. Non homogeneous but reducible to homogeneous equations
  5. Linear DE
  6. Non Linear but reducible to linear DE
  7. Exact equations
  8. Inexact equations but reducible to exact equation

Variable Separable Equations

If in an equation it is possible to separate all the functions of \(x\) and \(dx\) to one side and all the functions of \(y\) and \(dy\) to the other side then the variables are said to be separable. The general form of such equations is

Key Formula
\[f(x)dx = g(y)dy\]

Solution of variable separable equations is obtainable by integrating on both sides of the equation

Key Formula
\[\int f(x)dx = \int g(y)dy + c \text{ is the solution}\]
Example

Example: solve \((1 - x)dy + (1 - y)dx = 0\)

\((1 - x)dy = (y - 1)dx\)

\(\frac{1}{(y - 1)}dy = \frac{1}{(1 - x)}dx\)

\(\int \frac{1}{(1 - x)}dx = \int \frac{1}{(y - 1)}dy\)

\(-\log(1 - x) = \log(y - 1) + c\)

\(\log(y - 1) + \log(1 - x) + c = 0\)

\(\log[(1 - x)(y - 1)] = -c\)

\((1 - x)(y - 1) = e^{-c}\)

\((1 - x)(1 - y) = A\) where \(A = e^{-c}\) → general solution

Equations Reducible to Variable Separable

By choosing a suitable substitution some first order first degree differential equations are reducible to variable separable equations. Then we follow the same solution method of variable separable equations (i.e by integrating the equation).

Lecture 2: Solving First Order Differential Equations

First Order First Degree DE: Variable Separable

Variable Separable Method

Method: Separate variables such that the equation takes the form \(f(x)dx = f(y)dy\)

Example: Solve \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{x^2 + y^2}{x^2}\)

  1. Rewrite the equation as: \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 1 + \left(\frac{y}{x}\right)^2\)
  2. Let \(y = vx\), then \(\frac{dy}{dx} = v + x\frac{dv}{dx}\)
  3. Substitute into the equation: \(v + x\frac{dv}{dx} = 1 + v^2\)
  4. Separate variables: \(x\frac{dv}{dx} = 1 + v^2 - v\)
  5. Integrate both sides: \(\int \frac{dv}{1 + v^2 - v} = \int \frac{dx}{x}\)
  6. Solve the integral (result may be complex): \(\frac{2}{3} \log \left| \frac{(2v - 1)^2 + 3}{1}\right| + c_1 = \log|x| + c_2\)
  7. Substitute back \(v = y/x\) and simplify: \(\frac{2}{3} \log\left|\frac{(2y/x - 1)^2 + 3}{1}\right| = \log|x| + c\)

First Order First Degree DE: Reducible to Variable Separable

Example: Solve \(\frac{dy}{dx} = (4x + y + 1)^2\)

  1. Let \(4x + y + 1 = t\), then \(4 + \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dt}{dx}\)
  2. \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dt}{dx} - 4\)
  3. Substitute: \(\frac{dt}{dx} - 4 = t^2\)
  4. Separate variables: \(\frac{dt}{t^2 + 4} = dx\)
  5. Integrate: \(\int \frac{dt}{t^2 + 4} = \int dx\)
  6. \(\frac{1}{2} \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{t}{2}\right) = x + c\)
  7. Substitute back \(t = 4x + y + 1\): \(\frac{1}{2} \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{4x + y + 1}{2}\right) = x + c\)

First Order First Degree DE: Homogeneous

Homogeneous DE Method

Method: A differential equation is homogeneous if it can be written in the form \(\frac{dy}{dx} = f\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)\)

Example: Solve \(x^2 - y^2 = xy\frac{dy}{dx}\)

  1. Let \(y = vx\), then \(\frac{dy}{dx} = v + x\frac{dv}{dx}\)
  2. Substitute: \(x^2 - v^2x^2 = vx^2 \left(v + x\frac{dv}{dx}\right)\)
  3. Simplify and separate variables: \(\frac{1 - 2v^2}{v(1 + v^2)} dv = \frac{dx}{x}\)
  4. Integrate: \(\int \frac{1 - 2v^2}{v(1 + v^2)} dv = \int \frac{dx}{x}\)
  5. \(\frac{1}{2} \log |1 - v^2| = \log |x| + c\)
  6. Substitute back \(v = y/x\): \(\frac{1}{2} \log \left| 1 - \frac{y^2}{x^2}\right| = \log |x| + c\)

First Order First Degree DE: Non-Homogeneous Reducible to Homogeneous

Non-Homogeneous Method

Method: A differential equation is of this type if it can be written in the form \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{ax + by + c}{a'x + b'y + c'}\)

Reducible to Homogeneous Equations

Case 1: \(a_1/a_2 = b_1/b_2 = k\)

Let \( \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{a_1x + b_1y + c_1}{a_2x + b_2y + c_2} \)

If \(a_1/a_2 = b_1/b_2 = k\), then \( a_1 = ka_2 \) and \( b_1 = kb_2 \).

Let \( x = X + h \) and \( y = Y + k \).

Then,

\( \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{a_1(X+h) + b_1(Y+k) + c_1}{a_2(X+h) + b_2(Y+k) + c_2} \)

Choose \(h\) and \(k\) such that:

\( a_1h + b_1k + c_1 = 0 \)

\( a_2h + b_2k + c_2 = 0 \)

Then, \( \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{a_1X + b_1Y}{a_2X + b_2Y} = \frac{a_1X + kb_2Y}{a_2X + b_2Y} = \frac{ka_2X + kb_2Y}{a_2X + b_2Y} = k \frac{a_2X + b_2Y}{a_2X + b_2Y} = k \)

This is a homogeneous equation. Let \( Y = VX \), then \( \frac{dY}{dX} = V + X\frac{dV}{dX} \).

Case 2: \(a_1/a_2 = b_1/b_2 \neq c_1/c_2\)

Let \( \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2x+3y+4}{4x+6y+5} \)

Here \( \frac{2}{4} = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2} \neq \frac{4}{5} \)

Let \( 2x+3y = t \)

Then \( 2 + 3\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dt}{dx} \)

Substituting:

\( \frac{dt}{dx} - 2 = \frac{t+4}{2t+5/2} \)

\( \frac{dt}{dx} = \frac{3t+13}{2t+5} \)

Separating variables:

\( \int \frac{2t+5}{3t+13} dt = \int dx \)

Using partial fractions:

\( \frac{2t+5}{3t+13} = \frac{A}{3t+13} + B \)

\( 2t+5 = A + B(3t+13) \)

When \( t = -\frac{13}{3} \), \( -\frac{26}{3} + 5 = A \implies A = -\frac{11}{3} \)

When \( t = 0 \), \( 5 = A + 13B \implies 5 = -\frac{11}{3} + 13B \implies B = \frac{26}{39} = \frac{2}{3} \)

Then, \( \frac{2t+5}{3t+13} = \frac{2}{3} - \frac{11/3}{3t+13} \)

\( \int \left( \frac{2}{3} - \frac{11}{9t+39} \right) dt = x + c \)

First Order Linear Differential Equation

Definition: A first order linear differential equation is of the form \( \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x) \), where \( P(x) \) and \( Q(x) \) are functions of \( x \).

Multiply both sides by the integrating factor \( I.F. = e^{\int P(x) dx} \)

\( e^{\int P(x)dx} \left( \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y \right) = e^{\int P(x)dx} Q(x) \)

\( \frac{d}{dx} \left( ye^{\int P(x)dx} \right) = e^{\int P(x)dx} Q(x) \)

\( \int d \left( ye^{\int P(x)dx} \right) = \int e^{\int P(x)dx} Q(x) dx + c \)

\( ye^{\int P(x)dx} = \int e^{\int P(x)dx} Q(x) dx + c \)

Solution:

\( y e^{\int P(x)dx} = \int Q(x)e^{\int P(x)dx} dx + c \)

Example

Solve \( x\frac{dy}{dx} - y = x^2 e^x \)

  1. Dividing by \(x\):

    \( \frac{dy}{dx} - \frac{1}{x}y = xe^x \)

  2. Here \( P(x) = -\frac{1}{x} \) and \( Q(x) = xe^x \)

  3. Integrating factor \( = e^{\int -\frac{1}{x} dx} = e^{-\ln x} = \frac{1}{x} \)

  4. \( \frac{1}{x} \frac{dy}{dx} - \frac{1}{x^2} y = e^x \)

  5. \( \frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{y}{x} \right) = e^x \)

  6. \( \int d \left( \frac{y}{x} \right) = \int e^x dx + c \)

  7. \( \frac{y}{x} = e^x + c \)

  8. \( y = xe^x + cx \)

Non Linear Equation But Reducible to Linear (Bernoulli's Equation)

Definition: Bernoulli's equation is of the form \( \frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)y^n \), where \( n \neq 0, 1 \).

Divide by \( y^n \):

\( y^{-n}\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y^{1-n} = Q(x) \)

Let \( z = y^{1-n} \). Then \( \frac{dz}{dx} = (1-n)y^{-n}\frac{dy}{dx} \)

\( \frac{1}{1-n} \frac{dz}{dx} + P(x)z = Q(x) \)

\( \frac{dz}{dx} + (1-n)P(x)z = (1-n)Q(x) \)

This is a linear equation. The integrating factor is \( e^{\int (1-n)P(x) dx} \).

Solution:

\( z e^{\int (1-n)P(x)dx} = \int (1-n)Q(x)e^{\int (1-n)P(x)dx} dx + c \)

\( y^{1-n} e^{\int (1-n)P(x)dx} = \int (1-n)Q(x)e^{\int (1-n)P(x)dx} dx + c \)

Example

Solve \( x\frac{dy}{dx} + y = xy^2 \)

  1. Dividing by \( x \):

    \( \frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{1}{x}y = y^2 \)

  2. Dividing by \( y^2 \):

    \( y^{-2} \frac{dy}{dx} + \frac{1}{x}y^{-1} = 1 \)

  3. Let \( z = y^{-1} \). Then \( \frac{dz}{dx} = -y^{-2} \frac{dy}{dx} \)

  4. \( -\frac{dz}{dx} + \frac{1}{x}z = 1 \)

  5. \( \frac{dz}{dx} - \frac{1}{x}z = -1 \)

  6. I.F. \( = e^{\int -\frac{1}{x} dx} = \frac{1}{x} \)

  7. \( \frac{1}{x}\frac{dz}{dx} - \frac{1}{x^2} z = -\frac{1}{x} \)

  8. \( \frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{z}{x} \right) = -\frac{1}{x} \)

  9. \( \frac{z}{x} = -\ln x + c \)

  10. \( z = -x \ln x + cx \)

  11. \( \frac{1}{y} = -x \ln x + cx \)

  12. \( y = \frac{1}{-x \ln x + cx} \)
